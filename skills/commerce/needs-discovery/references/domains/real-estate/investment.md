# Investment Real Estate — Sub-Domain Reference

Constraints specific to buying property for appreciation or ROI (flip, hold,
develop). Referenced by `domains/real-estate/index.md`. Load in addition to
the generic real estate constraints.

## Investment Type Identification

Determine the investment strategy before analyzing constraints — each has
different risk profiles and key metrics:

| Strategy | Description | Key metric | Holding period | Risk |
|----------|-------------|-----------|----------------|------|
| **Fix and flip** | Buy, renovate, sell | ARV − (purchase + reno + carrying) | 3–12 months | Market timing, reno cost overruns |
| **Buy and hold** | Buy, rent out, hold for appreciation | Cap rate, cash flow | 5–30 years | Vacancy, tenant risk, market cycles |
| **BRRRR** | Buy, rehab, rent, refinance, repeat | Refinance appraisal | 6–18 months per cycle | Refinance risk, appraisal shortfall |
| **Development** | Buy land, build, sell or hold | Development cost vs end value | 1–5 years | Permitting, construction, market shift |
| **Wholesale** | Contract assignment, never take title | Assignment fee | 1–30 days | Buyer fall-through, legal risk |
| **Syndication / fund** | Pool capital for large deals | IRR, equity multiple | 5–10 years | Sponsor risk, illiquidity |

## Financial Analysis

### Cap Rate (Capitalization Rate)

```
Cap rate = Net Operating Income (NOI) / Purchase price
NOI = Gross rental income - operating expenses (excludes debt service, depreciation)
```

| Cap rate | Market context | Implication |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| < 4% | High-appreciation markets (SF, NYC) | Betting on appreciation, not cash flow |
| 4–6% | Balanced markets | Moderate cash flow, some appreciation |
| 6–8% | Cash flow markets (Midwest, South) | Strong cash flow, limited appreciation |
| 8–10%+ | High-risk or distressed markets | High cash flow but higher vacancy/tenant risk |

**Rule**: Cap rate alone is misleading. Compare to local market cap rates,
not national averages. A 7% cap rate in a declining market is worse than a
4% cap rate in an appreciating market.

### Cash Flow Analysis

```
Cash flow = NOI - debt service - capital expenditures - vacancy reserve
```

- **Vacancy reserve**: Budget 5–10% of gross income for vacancy + turnover
  costs (paint, cleaning, repairs between tenants)
- **CapEx reserve**: Budget 5–10% of gross income for major replacements
  (roof, HVAC, appliances)
- **Management**: 8–12% of collected rent if using a property manager

**Negative cash flow** (relying on appreciation to cover costs) is high
risk — if the market stalls, the investor bleeds monthly with no exit.

### Cash-on-Cash Return

```
Cash-on-cash = Annual cash flow / Total cash invested (down payment + closing + reno)
```

Target: 8–12%+ for buy-and-hold; 15–20%+ for flips (accounting for
shorter holding period).

### IRR (Internal Rate of Return)

For multi-year holds, IRR accounts for the time value of money. Target:
12–15%+ for residential investments, 15–20%+ for commercial/value-add.

## Market Analysis

- **Job growth**: Check Bureau of Labor Statistics for employment growth in
  the MSA. Strong job growth = rising demand = rising rents and values.
- **Population growth**: Census Bureau + state demographic data.
  Population growth drives housing demand.
- **Rent trends**: Check [Rentometer](https://www.rentometer.com/),
  [Apartment List](https://www.apartmentlist.com/), local MLS rent data.
  Are rents rising, flat, or falling?
- **Price-to-rent ratio**: `Home price / Annual rent`. Under 15 = good for
  buying; 15–20 = marginal; over 20 = better to rent. High ratio means
  you're betting on appreciation, not cash flow.
- **Supply**: Days on market, months of inventory, building permits issued.
  High supply = downward pressure on prices/rents.
- **Local economy diversification**: One-company towns (e.g., a factory
  town) are high risk — if the employer leaves, the market collapses.

## Exit Strategy

Before buying, identify at least two exit strategies:

| Strategy | When to use | Risk |
|----------|-------------|------|
| **Sell** | Market appreciates, or need to exit | Market timing, transaction costs |
| **Refinance** | Equity built via appreciation or reno | Appraisal risk, rate environment |
| **Hold and rent** | Cash flow positive, market stable | Long-term management burden |
| **1031 exchange** | Selling to buy another investment | Strict timeline (45 days to identify, 180 to close) |

**No exit strategy = no investment.** If the only way to make money is
appreciation and the market stalls, the investor is trapped.

## Risk Factors Specific to Investment

- **Over-leverage**: High debt-to-equity ratios amplify losses in a
  downturn. Recommended: keep DSCR (debt service coverage ratio) above
  1.25.
- **Renovation cost overruns**: Budget 15–20% contingency on renovations.
  Flippers routinely underestimate by 20–30%.
- **Carrying costs**: Mortgage, taxes, insurance, utilities during reno
  or vacancy. A 3-month delay on a flip can erase the profit margin.
- **Market timing risk**: Buying at the top of a cycle. Check price trends,
  inventory levels, and economic indicators. See
  `deal-intelligence/references/market-timing.md` for macro-economic
  signals.
- **Liquidity**: Real estate is illiquid. Plan for 3–12 months to sell.
  Don't invest money you might need in the short term.
