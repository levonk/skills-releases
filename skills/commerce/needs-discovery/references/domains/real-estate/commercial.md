# Commercial Real Estate — Sub-Domain Reference

Constraints specific to buying commercial property (retail, office,
industrial, mixed-use). Referenced by `domains/real-estate/index.md`. Load
in addition to the generic real estate constraints. Load alongside
`investment.md` and/or `rental.md` as applicable.

## Property Types

| Type | Description | Key risk |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Retail** | Strip malls, shopping centers, single-tenant | Tenant turnover, e-commerce disruption |
| **Office** | Multi-tenant or single-tenant office buildings | Remote work trend reducing demand |
| **Industrial** | Warehouses, manufacturing, flex space | Location relative to transport hubs |
| **Mixed-use** | Ground-floor retail + upper residential/office | Management complexity |
| **Hospitality** | Hotels, motels | Operating business, not just real estate |
| **Medical office** | Clinics, urgent care, medical condos | Specialized build-out, HIPAA compliance |
| **Self-storage** | Storage units | Low maintenance, climate-dependent demand |
| **Land** | Raw land, entitled land, development sites | Entitlement risk, holding costs |

## Environmental Due Diligence

Commercial real estate carries higher environmental liability than
residential. The Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and
Liability Act (CERCLA) makes the **current owner** liable for
contamination, even if they didn't cause it.

### Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (ESA)

Required before any commercial purchase. Cost: $2,000–$5,000. Reviews
historical use, records, and site reconnaissance to identify potential
contamination. If recognized environmental conditions (RECs) are found:

### Phase II ESA

If Phase I identifies RECs, a Phase II is required. Cost: $10,000–$50,000+.
Involves soil and groundwater sampling. If contamination is confirmed:

### Phase III — Remediation

Cleanup costs: $50,000–$1,000,000+. May be covered by the seller's
insurance or prior owner, but the current owner is liable if they walk
away.

**Rule**: Never buy commercial property without a Phase I ESA. If the
seller won't allow it, walk away.

## Zoning & Use

- **Commercial zoning categories**: Verify the specific commercial zoning
  (C-1 neighborhood commercial, C-2 general commercial, M-1 light
  industrial, M-2 heavy industrial). Each permits different uses.
- **Conditional use permits**: Some uses require a CUP even in commercially
  zoned areas (e.g., drive-through, 24-hour operation, alcohol sales).
- **Parking requirements**: Commercial zoning often requires minimum
  parking ratios (e.g., 1 space per 250 sq ft retail, 1 per 300 sq ft
  office). Non-conforming parking limits use.
- **Signage restrictions**: Sign size, illumination, and type may be
  regulated. This affects tenant visibility and lease value.

## Lease Types

| Lease type | What tenant pays | What landlord pays | Typical use |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Triple net (NNN)** | Base rent + taxes + insurance + maintenance | Building structure only | Retail, single-tenant |
| **Double net (NN)** | Base rent + taxes + insurance | Maintenance + structure | Multi-tenant retail |
| **Gross / full-service** | Base rent only | Everything else | Office |
| **Modified gross** | Base rent + some expenses (negotiated) | Some expenses (negotiated) | Office, mixed-use |
| **Industrial gross** | Base rent + utilities + janitorial | Taxes, insurance, structure | Industrial |

**NNN leases** are preferred by investors because the tenant pays most
operating expenses, making cash flow more predictable. But NNN leases
require the tenant to be financially strong — if the tenant defaults, the
landlord inherits all expenses.

## Tenant Creditworthiness

For single-tenant commercial properties, the tenant's financial strength
is everything:

- **National credit tenant** (Walgreens, McDonald's, Starbucks): Investment
  grade, long lease (10–20 years), minimal management. Lower cap rate
  (4–6%) because of lower risk.
- **Regional/local tenant**: Higher risk, shorter lease (3–5 years), may
  require more management. Higher cap rate (6–10%).
- **Personal guarantee**: For smaller tenants, require a personal guarantee
  from the business owner. This makes the owner personally liable for
  lease payments.

## ADA Compliance

Commercial properties must comply with the Americans with Disabilities Act:

- **Parking**: Accessible spaces required based on total spaces
- **Entrance**: Wheelchair-accessible entrance
- **Restrooms**: Accessible stalls, grab bars, clear floor space
- **Path of travel**: Wide enough for wheelchairs throughout

Non-compliance can result in lawsuits and mandatory remediation. Budget
$5,000–$50,000+ for ADA upgrades on older commercial buildings.

## Tenant Improvements (TI)

Commercial tenants often require build-out of the space. Negotiate who pays:

| TI scenario | Who pays | Impact on landlord |
|------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Turnkey** | Landlord pays all | Higher upfront cost, higher rent |
| **Tenant allowance ($/sq ft)** | Landlord pays up to $X/sq ft | Capped cost |
| **Tenant pays all** | Tenant | Lower rent, tenant takes improvement risk |

TI costs: $20–$100+/sq ft depending on build-out quality. Factor into the
lease economics.

## Commercial Financing

- **Down payment**: Typically 25–35% (vs 3–20% for residential)
- **Amortization**: 20–25 years (vs 30 for residential)
- **Balloon payment**: Many commercial loans balloon in 5–10 years,
  requiring refinance. Rate risk at refinance.
- **DSCR requirement**: Lenders typically require 1.25–1.35 debt service
  coverage ratio (NOI / debt service).
- **Lender types**: Banks (portfolio loans), CMBS (securitized, sold to
  investors), life insurance companies, private lenders. Each has
  different terms and risk appetite.
