# Rental Property — Sub-Domain Reference

Constraints specific to buying property to rent out (landlord perspective).
Referenced by `domains/real-estate/index.md`. Load in addition to the
generic real estate constraints. Load alongside `investment.md` if the
rental is also an investment (most common case).

## Landlord-Tenant Law

Landlord-tenant law varies significantly by state and city. Research the
specific jurisdiction before recommending a rental purchase.

### Eviction Process

| State category | Eviction timeline | Notes |
|---------------|------------------|-------|
| **Landlord-friendly** (TX, FL, GA, IN, AR) | 3–6 weeks | Fast eviction for non-payment; limited tenant protections |
| **Balanced** (most states) | 6–12 weeks | Standard notice + court process |
| **Tenant-friendly** (CA, NY, NJ, MA, OR, WA) | 3–12 months | Just-cause eviction required in many cities; tenant right to counsel; eviction moratoriums during emergencies |

**Key question**: How long does it take to evict a non-paying tenant in this
jurisdiction? In tenant-friendly states, a bad tenant can cost 6–12 months
of lost rent plus legal fees.

### Rent Control

| State | Rent control status |
|-------|-------------------|
| **California** | Statewide rent cap (CPI + 5%, max 10%/year); local ordinances may be stricter (SF, LA, Oakland) |
| **New York** | Statewide rent stabilization for buildings 6+ units built before 1974; NYC rent control for legacy tenants |
| **Oregon** | Statewide rent cap (CPI + 7%); no-cause eviction restrictions |
| **Washington** | Local rent control allowed (Seattle, Tacoma) |
| **New Jersey** | Strong rent control in many cities |
| **Most other states** | No rent control (but check local ordinances) |

**Rent control risk**: Buying a rent-controlled property with
below-market tenants means you cannot raise rents to market and may not be
able to recover costs. Check current rents vs market rents before buying.

### Security Deposit Limits

| State | Max deposit | Notes |
|-------|------------|-------|
| TX, FL, GA, most | 1–2 months rent | Landlord-friendly |
| CA | 2 months (unfurnished), 3 months (furnished) | |
| NY | 1 month (rent < $2,500) | |
| MA | 1 month | Plus first and last month = 3 months total |

### Required Disclosures

Most states require lead paint disclosure (pre-1978), mold disclosure,
death-in-property disclosure (varies by state), and specific disclosures
for known defects. Check state-specific requirements.

## Vacancy & Turnover

- **Vacancy rate**: Research the local market vacancy rate. National
  average: 5–7% for residential. High-vacancy areas (10%+) mean longer
  tenant searches and lower rents.
- **Turnover costs**: Each tenant turnover costs $1,000–$3,000 (paint,
  cleaning, minor repairs, marketing, lost rent during turnover). Budget
  for one turnover per year per unit.
- **Seasonal leasing**: Rentals listed in May–August lease faster and at
  higher rents (moving season). Rentals listed Nov–February take longer
  and may rent for less.

## Property Management

| Option | Cost | When to choose |
|--------|------|----------------|
| **Self-manage** | $0 (but your time) | Local property, few units, user has time |
| **Property manager** | 8–12% of collected rent | Out-of-area, many units, user wants passive |
| **Leasing-only service** | 0.5–1 month's rent | Find tenant, then self-manage |

Property manager selection: check references, verify license (required in
some states), review their lease agreement, understand their maintenance
markup (some charge 10–20% on top of contractor invoices).

## Tenant Screening

- **Credit check**: Minimum credit score (typically 620+ for residential)
- **Income verification**: 2.5–3x monthly rent in gross income
- **Rental history**: Verify with previous landlords (not just current —
  current landlord may want to get rid of them)
- **Background check**: Criminal history (follow Fair Housing Act — apply
  criteria consistently, don't discriminate)
- **Eviction history**: Check for prior evictions (strong predictor of
  future non-payment)

**Fair Housing Act compliance**: Never discriminate based on race, color,
national origin, religion, sex, familial status, or disability. Apply
screening criteria consistently to all applicants. Document the basis for
any rejection.

## Insurance

- **Landlord policy**: More expensive than homeowner's policy ($300–$800/yr
  more). Covers the building, liability, and loss of rental income.
- **Umbrella policy**: Recommended for landlords with multiple properties
  or significant assets ($150–$300/yr for $1M coverage).
- **Rent loss insurance**: Covers lost rent if the property is damaged and
  uninhabitable.
- **Flood insurance**: Required if in flood zone (separate policy via NFIP
  or private).

## Tax Considerations

- **Rental income is taxable**: Report on Schedule E (residential) or Form
  8825 (commercial). Deduct mortgage interest, property taxes, insurance,
  repairs, management fees, travel to the property.
- **Depreciation**: Depreciate the building (not land) over 27.5 years
  (residential) or 39 years (commercial). This is a major tax benefit —
  it can shelter rental income from taxes.
- **Depreciation recapture**: When you sell, depreciated amount is
  recaptured at 25% (federal). Factor this into exit strategy.
- **1031 exchange**: Defer capital gains by reinvesting in another
  investment property. Strict timeline: 45 days to identify, 180 days to
  close.
- **Passive activity loss rules**: Rental losses may be limited if the
  user's income is above $150,000 (MFJ). Consult a CPA.
