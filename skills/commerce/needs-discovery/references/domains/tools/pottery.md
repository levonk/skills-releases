# Pottery Tools — Constraints

What's unique to pottery equipment purchases. For generic power/battery/safety guidance, see `index.md`.

## Pottery Wheel

| Type | Price | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Electric tabletop | $500-2000 | Compact, portable, limited torque |
| Electric floor model | $1000-3500 | More torque, larger pieces |
| Kick wheel | $800-2000 | No motor, quiet, physical effort, reliable |

- Wheel head diameter: 11-14" — larger heads for larger pieces
- Rotation direction: most US wheels reverse (check if user is left-handed)
- Torque at low speed — critical for large pieces (cheap wheels bog down)
- Splash pan included? — if not, add $50-100

## Kiln

| Type | Price | Power | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Electric tabletop | $500-3000 | 120V | Small pieces, low-fire only |
| Electric standard | $1500-5000 | 240V | Full size, mid/high-fire |
| Gas | $2000-8000 | N/A | Reduction firing control |

- **Interior dimensions** — measure tallest piece + shelf + posts before buying
- Max temperature: Cone 6 (2232°F) for mid-fire stoneware; Cone 10 (2345°F) for high-fire
- 120V tabletop limited to small pieces and low-fire; **240V needs dedicated circuit** — confirm availability

## Kiln Furniture

Buy with kiln — needed immediately for first firing.

| Item | Price | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Shelves | $30-80 each | Semi-circular or square, match kiln interior |
| Posts | $1-5 each | Various heights for stacking |
| Stilts | $1-3 each | For glaze firing (keeps piece off shelf) |

## Clay

| Type | Cone Range | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Earthenware | Low-fire | Porous, needs glaze to be food-safe |
| Stoneware | Mid/high-fire | Durable, food-safe when vitrified |
| Porcelain | High-fire | Translucent when thin, challenging to throw |

- Cost: $15-40 per 25 lb bag
- Check cone range **matches kiln** — buying Cone 10 clay for a Cone 6 kiln wastes money

## Glazes

| Type | Price | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Commercial liquid | $15-40/pint | Ready to use, convenient |
| Dry glaze | $5-15/lb | Cheaper, mix yourself, bulk |

- Glaze chemistry: food-safe vs decorative only — **lead glazes are a food safety hazard, avoid**
- Test fire before committing to large batches — colors vary by firing temp and clay body

## Safety

- **Silica dust** from clay and glazes — respiratory hazard; use N95/P100 when mixing dry materials; clean with wet methods, NOT sweeping
- **Kiln ventilation** — electric kilns produce fumes from organics burning off and glaze materials; vent to outside or use kiln vent ($150-400)
- **Heat** — kiln surface reaches 400°F+ externally; keep flammables away; fire on non-combustible surface
- **Heavy metals in glazes** — barium, lithium, copper can be toxic; research safety data sheets before use

