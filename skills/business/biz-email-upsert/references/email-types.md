# Email Type Templates

Templates and worked examples for each business email type. Use these as
starting structures when drafting — adapt the content to the specific
situation. Apply intelligence about context before picking a type: the
situation, not just the noun in the user's request, determines the right
template.

## Situations Index

Real requests rarely arrive as a clean type name. Match the situation to a
type first, then apply the type's structure. When a request spans more than
one type, split it into separate emails (principle 6).

| Situation | Type |
|-----------|------|
| Asking someone to do something | Action Needed |
| Asking someone to choose between options | Decision Needed |
| Informing stakeholders of status | Update |
| Flagging a problem or potential issue | Risk |
| Raising an issue to a higher authority | Escalation |
| Verifying an agreement or understanding | Confirmation |
| Emailing an investor, journalist, or cold contact for the first time | Cold Outreach |
| Requesting, making, or following up on an introduction | Introduction |
| Building a relationship with a new contact (not a specific ask) | Networking |
| Saying no, declining, or rejecting | Refusal / Rejection |
| Resigning from a job | Resignation |
| Punting a vague email back for specifics | Clarification Request |

## Action Needed

Request someone to do something.

### Structure

```
Subject: Action needed [deadline]: [what needs doing]

[What you need the recipient to do, in one sentence.]

[Two to three lines of context: why it matters, what's blocked, what depends on it.]

[Specific action requested + deadline.]
```

### Example

```
Subject: Action needed today: approve revised offer

Please approve the revised offer for Client X so we can send it by 5 pm.

Legal flagged two clauses that need adjustment (sections 3.2 and 4.1). The
revised draft is attached. Client X expects the offer by tomorrow morning.

Reply with "approved" or requested changes by 3 pm today.
```

## Decision Needed

Request a choice between options.

### Structure

```
Subject: Decision needed [deadline]: [what needs deciding]

[The decision that needs to be made, in one sentence.]

[Two to five lines: the options with facts, names, numbers, dates.]

[Your recommendation and why.]

[Who decides + deadline.]
```

### Example

```
Subject: Decision needed: vendor delay on onboarding

Vendor X missed the March 12 deadline. If we keep the current scope, onboarding
moves to March 19.

Options:
1. Keep full scope, launch March 19 (one week delay)
2. Remove feature Y, launch March 15 (on time, reduced scope)

My recommendation is option 2 — feature Y is nice-to-have and the March 15
date was committed to the client.

Please reply by 3 pm today so we can confirm with the client.
```

## Update

Inform stakeholders of status.

### Structure

```
Subject: Update: [what's updated]

[The headline: what changed, in one sentence.]

[Two to five lines: details with names, numbers, dates.]

[Next milestone or next update timing.]
```

### Example

```
Subject: Update: contract signed with Acme

Acme signed the contract today. The engagement starts March 1 with a 6-month
initial term, value $180K.

Kickoff meeting is scheduled for March 3 at 10 am. I'll send the agenda by
Friday.

No action needed — this is for your awareness.
```

## Risk

Flag a problem or potential issue.

### Structure

```
Subject: Risk: [what's at risk]

[The risk, in one sentence. Bad news first.]

[Two to five lines: what happened, impact, likelihood.]

[Recommendation or mitigation + owner + deadline.]
```

### Example

```
Subject: Risk: campaign under target this week

The campaign is 18 percent below target after four days.

Likely cause is narrow audience targeting. At current rate, we'll miss the
monthly target by 12 percent.

Recommendation: expand targeting today and review performance again tomorrow
at 2 pm. I'll own the adjustment and send an update by end of day.
```

## Escalation

Raise an issue to a higher authority.

### Structure

```
Subject: Escalation: [what's escalated]

[The issue and why it's escalated, in one sentence.]

[Two to five lines: what was tried, what failed, what's blocked.]

[What you need from this person + deadline.]
```

### Example

```
Subject: Escalation: engineering resource conflict on Project X

Project X is blocked because the two assigned engineers were pulled to
incident response on Project Y. I've raised this with the Y lead but no
resolution yet.

Project X's deadline is March 15. At current staffing, we'll miss it by 5
days. I need either the engineers back by Monday or approval to contract a
temporary replacement.

Can you decide by end of day Friday so we can adjust the plan?
```

## Confirmation

Verify an agreement or understanding.

### Structure

```
Subject: Confirmation: [what's confirmed]

[What was agreed, in one sentence.]

[Two to three lines: specifics — who, what, when, where.]

[Ask for confirmation or corrections.]
```

### Example

```
Subject: Confirmation: Q2 budget allocation

Following our meeting today, confirming the Q2 budget allocation:

- Engineering: $120K (3 hires)
- Marketing: $45K (campaign + tools)
- Operations: $30K (infra + vendor renewals)

Please reply with "confirmed" or corrections by Wednesday so I can finalize
the plan.
```

## Cold Outreach

Email an investor, journalist, or cold contact for the first time. The goal is
a reply, not a meeting — let the recipient escalate. This type does **not** use
the triage-tag subject pattern; the subject describes what you do.

### Structure

```
Subject: [what you do, in plain words]

[One sentence: what you do, no jargon.]

[One sentence: why they should care — market, traction, growth, or notable
founders. Not your resume or personal story.]

[One sentence: the ask — advice, investment, or an introduction. Do not ask
for a call or meeting.]
```

### Rules

- Send from your @company.com email, not a personal address.
- Do not chase a warm intro first — email directly. A direct, well-written
  cold email beats a delayed warm intro.
- Do not compliment the recipient or persuade them to help. They already want
  to help; your job is to make it easy.
- Do not attach a deck or business plan in the first email. Offer it if they
  ask.

### Example

```
Subject: Twitch for cooking

Hi Michael,

I'm building Twitch for cooking. We have 50,000 uploads a day and 20 percent
month-over-month growth for the past four months, and we're raising a seed
round.

Would you be open to taking a look? Happy to send the deck if useful.

Thanks,
Tim
```

## Introduction

Request, make, or follow up on an introduction. This is a multi-party workflow
with three roles (requester, introducer, target), not a single email. Use
**double opt-in**: ask both parties before sending the intro email, unless you
have a trusted relationship with the target and are confident they welcome it.

### Sub-scenario 1: Requesting an Introduction

You want someone to introduce you to a third party.

#### Structure

```
Subject: Intro request: [target name] — [one-line reason]

[One sentence: thank them for offering to introduce you and say you'll send a
fresh email they can forward.]

[Then send a SEPARATE fresh email — not a reply — containing everything the
target should see: what you do, why it's relevant, traction, and the ask. Keep
it short and forwardable from a phone.]
```

#### Rules

- Send a fresh, forwardable email — never ask the introducer to edit a long
  thread. They should be able to forward it from their phone with one tap.
- The fresh email is the one the target will read. Write it for them, not for
  the introducer.
- Obey the Cold Outreach rules inside the forwardable email: short, clear,
  specific ask.

#### Example (the forwardable email)

```
Mark,

Thanks for offering to introduce me to Suzanne. I'll send a fresh email now
that you can forward on.

Best,
Joe
```

```
Subject: Instacolor — intro for Suzanne

Suzanne,

Mark suggested we connect. I'm building Instacolor — Instagram meets Color
but for video. We're at 50,000 uploads a day, growing 20 percent month over
month for four months, and raising a seed round.

I'd value your perspective on the seed round. Happy to send the deck if
useful.

Best,
Joe the Founder
```

### Sub-scenario 2: Making an Introduction

You are the introducer. **Never CC both parties cold** — it traps the target.
Forward the requester's email with an opt-in request on top so the target can
decline gracefully.

#### Structure

```
Subject: Fwd: [requester's subject]

[One or two sentences: would you like to be introduced? Add a brief endorsement
if you have one, or just pass it along neutrally.]

[The forwarded email from the requester appears below.]
```

#### Rules

- Double opt-in: ask the target before sending the intro. If you have a
  trusted, daily relationship with the target, a direct intro is acceptable —
  use judgment, not a rigid rule.
- If the target declines, you handle the awkwardness — that is the point of
  the protocol. Be choosy about who you introduce and declinations stay rare.
- A neutral forward ("Joe asked for an intro, see below") is fine when you
  cannot honestly endorse. An enthusiastic forward ("worth your time, see
  below") is better when you can.

#### Example

```
Suzanne,

Would you like to be introduced to Joe? He runs Instacolor and I think it's
worth your time. See below.

Best,
Mark
```

### Sub-scenario 3: After the Introduction Is Made

The introducer has connected you. You (the requester) respond and move the
introducer to BCC.

#### Structure

```
[One line: thank the introducer and note they are moved to BCC.]

[One or two lines: engage the target directly — propose a next step or ask
your question.]
```

#### Rules

- Move the introducer to BCC on your first reply. They are no longer needed
  in the thread and should not be copied on the ongoing conversation.
- Do not make the target wait — reply promptly with a concrete next step.

#### Example

```
Thanks Mark (moved to bcc).

Suzanne, great to meet you. Are you available for a 20-minute call Thursday
at 2 pm, or Friday morning? Happy to send the deck ahead of time.

Best,
Joe
```

## Networking

Build a relationship with a new contact when there is no specific ask yet.
Distinct from Cold Outreach (which has a concrete ask) and Introduction (which
goes through a mutual contact). Use this for direct, low-pressure outreach to
someone you want to know.

### Structure

```
Subject: [what connects you — a shared interest, event, or context]

[One sentence: who you are and the specific thing that connects you.]

[One or two sentences: what you find interesting about their work, with a
specific reference — not generic praise.]

[One sentence: a soft, low-cost next step — a question they can answer in two
lines, or an offer to share something useful. No meeting request.]
```

### Rules

- Reference something specific they wrote, said, or did. Generic "I admire
  your work" emails read as spam.
- Make the next step cost them almost nothing — a reply, not a call.
- If you want a meeting, earn it over one or two exchanges first.

### Example

```
Subject: Your post on async standups

Hi Priya,

I read your post on async standups and the "written-first" cadence resonated
— we're moving our team to async next month and the cadence breakdown was the
piece I was missing.

One question: did you keep the synchronous standup for incident weeks, or go
fully async? Happy to share our runbook once it's settled if it's useful.

Best,
Dan
```

## Refusal / Rejection

Say no — decline a project, turn down a candidate, reject an offer, or refuse
a request. Be direct, brief, and respectful. Do not over-explain or hedge.

### Structure

```
Subject: [Refusal / Rejection]: [what is being declined]

[One sentence: the no, stated directly. No softening preamble.]

[One or two sentences: the reason, in factual terms. Not a long justification.]

[One sentence: a door-left-open line if genuine, or a clean close if not. Do
not offer false hope.]
```

### Rules

- Say no in the first sentence. Do not bury it behind context or gratitude.
- Give a real reason, not a vague "not a fit." If you cannot share the reason,
  say so explicitly rather than fabricating one.
- Keep the door open only if you mean it. "Happy to revisit in Q3" is fine;
  "maybe someday" is not.
- For candidates: be prompt. Silence is worse than a fast no.

### Example (declining a project)

```
Subject: Rejection: Project Phoenix proposal

We won't be taking on Project Phoenix this quarter. The scope overlaps with
two commitments we already have through March, and we can't staff it without
slipping those.

If the timeline moves to Q2, I'd be glad to look at it again.

Best,
Sam
```

### Example (rejecting a candidate)

```
Subject: Update on your application — Engineering Manager

Thanks for taking the time to interview with us. We won't be moving forward
with your application. The team was impressed by your work on the system
design round; we went with a candidate whose background matched our
infrastructure stack more closely.

We'd welcome another application from you for future roles that open up.

Best,
The Hiring Team
```

## Resignation

Resign from a job. Formal, brief, and unambiguous. This is a legal record, not
a venue for grievances or a farewell.

### Structure

```
Subject: Resignation — [your name]

[One sentence: I am resigning from my role as [title], effective [date].]

[One or two sentences: the notice period and your last working day.]

[One sentence: offer to support the handoff. Keep it short and professional.]
```

### Rules

- State the resignation and the effective date in the first sentence. No
  preamble, no gratitude paragraph first.
- Give the notice required by your contract or local law.
- Do not air grievances in the resignation email. If you have feedback, give
  it separately in an exit interview.
- Send it to your direct manager. HR can be CC'd if your company requires it.

### Example

```
Subject: Resignation — Jordan Lee

I'm resigning from my role as Senior Engineer, effective March 1. My last
working day will be March 15, giving the standard two weeks' notice.

I'll work with you on a handoff plan for the payments service and the two
in-flight migrations. Let me know what's most useful.

Best,
Jordan
```

## Clarification Request

Punt a vague email back to the sender for specifics. Use when you receive an
email that is long on words and short on a clear ask.

### Structure

```
Subject: Re: [original subject] — clarification needed

[One sentence: state what you need to understand before you can respond.]

[One or two sentences: list the specific questions, as a short numbered list if
there is more than one.]

[One sentence: a go-forward plan — "I'll respond within [timeframe] once I
have these," or "if easier, a 10-minute call Wednesday works."]
```

### Rules

- Do not guess what they meant and answer the wrong question. Ask first.
- Be specific about what's missing — names, dates, the decision they want,
  the action they're requesting.
- Offer a low-cost alternative (a short call) if the email is too tangled to
  untangle asynchronously.
- Do not make the clarification itself a burden — keep your questions short
  and answerable in a few lines.

### Example

```
Subject: Re: Quick question — clarification needed

Before I can answer, I need to know which deployment you're referring to —
the staging push from Tuesday or the production release planned for Friday?

1. Which environment is affected?
2. Is the issue blocking the Friday release, or is it a separate concern?

Once I have those, I'll respond within the hour. If easier, I'm free for a
10-minute call at 3 pm today.

Best,
Alex
```
